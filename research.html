Price Discrimination, Product Differentiation, and Gender-Based Pricing in Consumer Packaged Goods
Job Market Paper, with Yvette Zhang

Abstract: This paper examines the economic mechanisms underlying persistent price gaps between products marketed to women and men, which is often referred as the "Pink Tax". Using NielsenIQ scanner data on toothbrushes, we provide systematic evidence of gendered price differentials and analyze two mechanisms central to the literature: third-degree price discrimination and product differentiation. We first test for price discrimination by estimating own-price elasticities and exploiting a natural experiment created by New York State’s 2020 legislation banning gender-based pricing. Our results show that women’s products face significantly lower demand elasticities, and the policy intervention reduced but did not eliminate price gaps, consistent with price discrimination literature. We then estimate a random-coefficient demand model to recover marginal costs and markups. These structural results reveal that women’s products are positioned in ways that reduce substitution pressure, leading to higher markups despite lower marginal costs. This pattern indicates that product differentiation, not cost differences, sustains the persistent price disparities. Taken together, our findings show that price discrimination and product differentiation jointly determine gender-based pricing outcomes and that regulatory efforts aimed at banning price discrimination alone may have limited effectiveness when firms also rely on differentiation to sustain markups.

Educational Assortative Matching and Income Inequality in China
With Song Zhou and Yvette Zhang

Abstract:This paper studies how educational assortative matching affects household income inequality in China between 2010 and 2018. During this period, people increasingly married partners with similar levels of education. Using data from the Chinese General Social Survey (CGSS), we combine counterfactual re-matching simulations with models of female labor supply to quantify the distributional effects of marital sorting. We find that educational assortative matching makes a substantial contribution to income inequality. In 2018, observed matching explains about 53\% of the gap between random matching and perfect income-based matching. In contrast to evidence from developed economies, female labor supply plays only a limited role in amplifying inequality in China. Changes in women’s labor force participation and working hours have negligible effects on household income inequality. Instead, we document a synchronization effect within households. Wives’ labor supply does not respond to husbands’ income but is strongly correlated with husbands’ working hours. As a result, assortative matching creates high-income dual-earner households whose labor inputs move together. These findings suggest that in China’s high female participation economy, inequality is driven mainly by the concentration of human capital at marriage rather than by post-marital labor supply adjustments.
